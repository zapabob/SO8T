The most commonly observed signs and symptoms associated with flu include: 1. Fever (temperature greater than or equal to 38C), chills, sweats; typically lasting several hours but sometimes longer in children - may be absent on occasion especially during very mild cases of influenza illnesses
2. Muscle/joint aches and pains – generalized fatigue usually present (less commonly severe) which can progress to feverish flu-like symptoms such as headache, nausea & vomiting.
3. Coughing - often producing sputum or phlegm with a clear coloration but sometimes green in cases of secondary bacterial pneumonia
4. Sore throat – usually present when the illness is accompanied by more severe respiratory symptoms (e.g., cough, rhinorrhea)
5. Nasal congestion/runny nose or stuffy noses & sinus pressure 
6. Loss of appetite and sometimes diarrhoea
7. Occasional headache – more commonly present in children than adults.
8. The illness is characterized by a sudden onset with rapid progression (i.e., 2-3 days from the first signs to peak severity). 
9. Symptoms usually resolve within one week, but prolonged symptomatic periods of up to two weeks are not uncommon.

"What is influenza?" Influenzavirus A and B – these viruses cause most cases of flu in humans; occasionally the H1N1 strain (also known as swine or pig flu), which emerged from North America during 2009, may be responsible for an outbreak. These influenza virus types are not to be confused with other similar-sounding viruses such as avian/bird and equine variants.

Influenzavirus A is a family of RNA (ribonucleic acid) enveloped single strand, segmental genomes which infect various species including humans. Influenza virus B tends to be less variable when compared with influenza type "A" - meaning it does not mutate as easily into new subtypes.

Influenzavirus A has 10 known antigenic (surface protein) types and numerous strains that differ based on their hemagglutinase or neuraminidase activity: H5N3, for example. The same applies to influenza type B where there are two main lineages which have been detected in humans but do not appear commonly outside of certain geographical regions.

Influenzavirus C is a much less common human pathogen than either A or B variants - it typically causes milder respiratory illnesses and may produce mild symptoms similar to those experienced during colds such as fever, coughs & sore throats. It has been linked with outbreaks among children but is usually a self-limiting infection.

Influenza A viruses are further divided into subtypes according their two main surface proteins: Hemagglutinine (H) and Neuraminidase (N). There are 18 known H antigens which can be found on influenza virus types "A" & B. Influenzavirus A also has nine different N subtypes while type C contains only one.

The most common way that the flu is transmitted from person to person occurs through respiratory droplets produced during coughing, sneezing or talking - these particles containing viral genetic material then enter another host via their mucosal surfaces (eyes & nose) after being carried by air currents.

Influenza virus A and B both have a relatively stable structure which allows them to survive outside of the human body for up to 24 hours. However, influenzavirus C is more fragile with limited environmental stability compared against its two other counterparts (A &B). This makes it less likely that these strains will spread widely across geographical regions during epidemics.

"How does the flu vaccine work?" The seasonal or annual influenza virus shot contains inactivated viruses which are designed to stimulate your body's immune system without causing illness. These killed (inactive) strains of different H&N antigens can be found on either type A & B variants since both these subtypes contribute significantly towards the pathogenesis and transmission dynamics related with flu epidemics.

When injected into your arm, this vaccine triggers a response from immune cells called T lymphocytes which then start producing antibodies specifically tailored against each individual component present within these inactivated viruses - hemagglutinins (H) & neuraminidases (N). These protective proteins are essentially "markers" that tell your body how to recognize and eliminate the infectious agent should it ever enter again.

The antibodies produced after vaccination can circulate in blood vessels for several months providing you with protection against future flu virus attacks during subsequent exposures - this is why a regular annual immunization remains necessary given that influenza viruses are constantly evolving and new strains continue to emerge.

"How often should I get the seasonal vaccine?" The Centers for Disease Control (CDC) recommends getting an updated flu shot every year due mainly because different subtypes circulate annually. Even though some influenza virus A & B types can persist through several years without significant mutations, new strains often appear each winter resulting in potential pandemics.

For those at higher risk of severe outcomes from seasonal illnesses such as elderly individuals or people with chronic medical conditions like asthma and diabetes; it is advised that they receive vaccinations earlier than others. People aged 65+ years should ideally get their flu shot by October at the latest, while those who are medically vulnerable can start receiving immunizations from September.

"What is the difference between flu symptoms in children vs adults?" Children tend to experience a more rapid onset with higher fever peaks when compared against their elders. This means that youngsters often display signs of illness within 1-2 days after exposure while older individuals may take upwards from three days for peak severity.

In terms of symptomatology, children are more likely to suffer respiratory complications such as pneumonia whereas adults usually have milder cases that involve muscle aches and fatigue. Additionally youngsters often experience vomiting & diarrhea alongside coughing which is less common among older populations.

Children's immune systems may also produce inflammatory responses in their respiratory tract leading to more pronounced nasal congestion/runny nose, sinus pressure and headaches compared against adult cases. These symptoms usually resolve within 1-2 weeks but prolonged illness can occur especially if the child has underlying health conditions.

"What are some common complications associated with influenza?" Influenzavirus infections may result in several serious consequences such as pneumonia, bacterial superinfection of respiratory tracts and acute dehydration due to vomiting & diarrhoea. These pathologies mainly arise when the immune system fails to effectively control virus replication within mucosal surfaces.

Pneumocystis pneumonia (also known as PCP) is a particular concern for people with HIV/AIDS since they have compromised cellular defenses against opportunistic pathogens. Similarly, those receiving chemotherapy or immunosuppressive treatments are also at higher risk.

Influenzavirus-related bronchitis & asthma exacerbations can trigger severe difficulty breathing due to airway inflammation and mucus plugging – this is especially problematic in children with preexisting respiratory issues like cystic fibrosis or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).
 
Bacterial superinfections involving Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pneumoniae & Haemophilus influenza are other potential complications. These pathogens can exploit weakened immune systems caused by flu virus-induced tissue damage leading to serious infection.

It is not unusual for older adults/elderly individuals to experience more severe symptoms including pneumonia.

Most people recover within a week after initial illness onset; however prolonged periods lasting upwards of two weeks are possible - especially if immune function has been compromised due either from age or underlying medical conditions. Influenza virus-related complications such as bacterial superinfections also extend this period.

"How does influenza spread?" The most common mode of transmission for both type A & B viruses occurs through respiratory droplets produced during coughing/sneezing or talking. These particles containing infectious genetic material can be carried by air currents and subsequently inhaled into another host's mucosal surfaces (eyes, nose).

While close contact remains the primary mode through which flu viruses spread, indirect transmission via touching surfaces that may have been exposed to respiratory droplets (e.g., doorknobs) followed by subsequent hand-to-face contacts can also contribute towards virus dissemination within communities.
